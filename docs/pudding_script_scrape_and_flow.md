### A) Women’s Sizing (`Sizing chaos`) - Emotional + systemic narrative

- Story feeling:
  - personal memory + identity + recurring frustration,
  - then zoom out to population-level structural problem.
- Script moves to mirror:
  - start with a relatable character lens,
  - transition to percentile/population framing,
  - conclude that pain is structural, not individual failure.
- Tone profile:
  - empathetic, validating, lightly indignant.
- Writing pattern:
  - concrete body/life details first, sizing system critique second.

### B) Sitters and Standers - Binary framing + cultural divide

- Story feeling:
  - clean two-group framing (stand vs sit),
  - almost identity-politics but for labor/body posture.
- Script moves to mirror:
  - establish an instantly legible binary,
  - let audience self-locate quickly ("which side am I on?"),
  - use that binary to expose class/power/autonomy differences.
- Tone profile:
  - concise, declarative, accessible.
- Writing pattern:
  - short scene setup, then high-contrast comparisons.

### C) Teens Into Adults (`This is a teenager`) - Longitudinal human arc

- Story feeling:
  - one-person anchor narrative that expands into cohort data.
- Script moves to mirror:
  - open with named/defined archetype,
  - follow timeline progression ("today" -> "years later"),
  - layer structural risk variables without losing personhood.
- Tone profile:
  - compassionate, sober, future-oriented.
- Writing pattern:
  - alternating personal beat -> cohort statistic -> interpretation.

### D) EU Regions Borders - Policy explainer through one concrete case

- Story feeling:
  - starts technical, but explained through a specific country split.
- Script moves to mirror:
  - anchor with one concrete case before full mechanism,
  - explain system rules in plain language,
  - return to map-level implications.
- Tone profile:
  - analytic but readable; policy without jargon overload.
- Writing pattern:
  - "example -> system logic -> broader pattern -> implications."

### E) Strangers Talking (`30 minutes with a stranger`) - Real-time social experiment

- Story feeling:
  - present-tense "watch this unfold" storytelling.
- Script moves to mirror:
  - set up real participants and a timed interaction,
  - contrast predicted discomfort vs measured outcomes,
  - connect findings to broader social-trust decline.
- Tone profile:
  - curious, intimate, behaviorally grounded.
- Writing pattern:
  - moment-by-moment narration + interpretive pauses.

### What all five share (the vibe DNA to copy)

1. Open with a person or concrete scene, not a dataset.
2. Make the reader feel a tension before explaining method.
3. Move from micro (individual case) to macro (system pattern).
4. Use interaction cues as story beats, not UI instructions.
5. End with structural implication, not just an isolated fact.

### Mirror Constraints For Our Script Rewrite

When rewriting our story, enforce:

1. First 2 paragraphs must contain zero methodological jargon.
2. Every chapter begins with either:
   - a person-level vignette, or
   - a concrete contradiction.
3. Every chart scene must answer one emotional question:
   - "What feels unfair/confusing/surprising here?"
4. At least one chapter uses a timeline progression voice ("then, later, now").
5. Methods are delayed until reader motivation is established.

## Typical Pudding Script Flow (Observed Pattern)

This is the recurring script shape across the corpus.

1. **Tension Hook**
   - Start with a contradiction, pain point, or provocative question.
   - Goal: immediate narrative tension, not method detail.

2. **Why This Matters**
   - Translate topic into human, civic, cultural, or identity stakes.
   - Goal: audience buys in before charts get complex.

3. **Orient the Reader**
   - Quick interaction cue: what to look at, click, or scroll for.
   - Goal: reduce friction and cognitive load.

4. **First Evidence Reveal**
   - One strong visual + one clear claim.
   - Goal: establish credibility and momentum.

5. **Escalation Through Chapters**
   - Alternate between:
     - pattern,
     - explanation,
     - edge case/person-level example.
   - Goal: avoid monotonous "chart dump."

6. **Method Transparency at the Right Moment**
   - Explain enough method to trust the finding, not full appendix in-line.
   - Goal: maintain pace while preserving rigor.

7. **Complication / Caveat**
   - Acknowledge what the first interpretation misses.
   - Goal: increase trust and nuance.

8. **Resolution + Reflection**
   - End with a sharpened takeaway and broader implication.
   - Goal: reader leaves with a changed mental model.

## Emotional Engine Behind the Flow

Common emotional sequence:

1. Curiosity
2. Recognition ("I know this problem")
3. Surprise ("I didn't expect that pattern")
4. Tension ("what explains this?")
5. Agency (interactive exploration)
6. Reflection (what this means now)

Common craft choices that create that sequence:

- direct second-person voice ("you")
- concrete nouns before abstract claims
- short sentence bursts near transitions
- frequent contrast words ("but", "however", "yet")
- human examples inserted between analytical blocks

## Script Quality Rubric (Pudding-Style)

Score each section 1-5.

1. **Hook Strength**
   - Does the first paragraph create tension immediately?

2. **Stake Clarity**
   - Is it clear why this matters beyond data novelty?

3. **Narrative Progression**
   - Do sections build on each other instead of repeating chart commentary?

4. **Visual-Text Coupling**
   - Does each paragraph tell the reader what to notice in the current visual?

5. **Emotional Texture**
   - Is there voice, consequence, and human relevance (not sterile reporting)?

6. **Method Trust**
   - Are methods and caveats present at the points readers need them?

7. **Ending Quality**
   - Does the conclusion synthesize, not just restate?

## Reusable Script Skeleton (For Our Project)

Use this exact sequence for first rewrite:

1. **Hook paragraph**
   - "If wealth alone determined development outcomes, we would expect..."

2. **Stake paragraph**
   - "This matters because..."

3. **Chart orientation**
   - "Each dot is..., the line represents..., color means..."

4. **Primary reveal**
   - "The pattern is..., but the exception is..."

5. **Comparison chapter**
   - "Countries with similar income show..."

6. **Complication chapter**
   - "The first interpretation misses..."

7. **Trend chapter**
   - "Over time, trajectories diverge..."

8. **Conclusion**
   - "Wealth enables X, but Y determines whether..."

9. **Methods + caveat block**
   - concise bullet list with known limitations.

## Practical Takeaways For Your Rewrite

- Lead with one hard tension, not a dataset description.
- Treat each chart as a scene with a point, not a figure with narration.
- Keep method details compressed in the narrative path; move depth to method cards.
- Write transitions like story beats ("Now zoom in...", "But this breaks down when...").
- End each chapter with a reason to continue (a small unresolved question).
