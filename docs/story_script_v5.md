# Story Script V5 (EU-Length Depth, Single-Mechanism Arc)

## Title / Dek Options

### Option A
- **Title:** Progress Is Governable
- **Dek:** Income draws a line. Health systems decide the distance from it.

### Option B
- **Title:** Wealth Doesn't Buy Health. Why not?
- **Dek:** Across 217 countries, income predicts a lot. The remaining gap is where institutions matter most.

---

[VISUAL: Empty scatter axes. X-axis = income per person. Y-axis = life expectancy. No dots yet.]

Before any country appears, start with the pattern.

Richer countries usually live longer. That is one of the most stable patterns in global development data.

[VISUAL: All 2010 country dots fade in together, unlabeled.]

Now hold that picture for a second.

If this were a perfect world, every dot would sit on a smooth curve: one level of income, one expected level of survival. But real countries do not line up that neatly.

[VISUAL: Trend line draws over the cloud. Hover reveals vertical distance from each dot to the line.]

In 2010, income explains about two-thirds of cross-country variation in life expectancy in this dataset. That is substantial. It is also incomplete.

That remaining third is where countries with similar income end up with very different life chances.

Treat the line as a benchmark, not a verdict. The key quantity is the vertical distance between a country and that line.

Above the line means people are living longer than income alone predicts.
Below the line means they are living shorter lives than income alone predicts.

[VISUAL: A horizontal distribution strip appears under the chart, centered at zero gap.]

This is where scale matters.

A typical country is not exactly on the line. In 2010, the median absolute life-expectancy gap is about 2.7 years. So modest divergence is normal.

But the tails are not modest. One in ten countries sits more than about 7.8 years away from expectation. At the extremes, the spread is wider still: around +11 years on the high side and about -18 years on the low side.

Here is the premise:
income is powerful,
income is not destiny,
and the gap is big enough to matter in real lives.

Once that frame is clear, we can track countries the way this chart tracks them: not by rank alone, but by distance from expectation and movement over time.

[VISUAL: Cut to Rwanda only. Large text card: "Under-5 mortality, Rwanda: 2006 = 98.3 per 1,000."]

2006: 98.3.

[VISUAL: Hold. No narration.]

[VISUAL: Card updates: "2015 = 42.0 per 1,000."]

2015: 42.0.

[VISUAL: Hold. No narration.]

That drop is the first fact.

The second fact is where this period starts. Rwanda enters the 2006-2015 window after a decade of state and health-system reconstruction following the mid-1990s collapse. The baseline is not a mature, fully resourced system. It is a country rebuilding capacity while still carrying severe mortality burden.

So the movement in this decade is not just "improvement happened." It is improvement happening from a difficult starting point, and happening across multiple frontline outcomes at once.

In this dataset:
maternal mortality drops from 534 to 290,
infant mortality drops from 62.6 to 31.4,
under-5 mortality drops from 98.3 to 42.0,
and life expectancy rises from 57.2 to 66.7.

[VISUAL: Rwanda dot path animates across years against a fixed income-life line. Gap labels are shown on chart annotation, not in prose.]

More important than one endpoint is the trajectory relative to expectation. Rwanda starts slightly below the income-based line and moves above it by 2010, then farther above by 2015.

That does not prove one policy caused one outcome.

It does show the gap can move in the favorable direction for a sustained stretch, not only in a one-year spike.

And it shows what "institutions matter" looks like in practice: the same income level can deliver different survival odds depending on how care is financed, organized, staffed, and reached by households.

[VISUAL: Vietnam dot appears on the same chart. Rwanda and Vietnam both highlighted above the line.]

Rwanda is not the only dot on that side.

In 2010, Vietnam sits roughly 11 years above its income-based life-expectancy expectation.

Vietnam comes from a different regional and institutional path, but the signal is the same: above the line.

Vietnam is also not a one-year outlier in this series. It starts the decade far above the line and remains far above it by 2015.

That brief confirmation matters because it lowers the chance that Rwanda is a one-case narrative. We are looking at a pattern that can appear in more than one place, through more than one pathway.

[VISUAL: Rwanda and Vietnam fade. U.S. dot highlights. Neighboring high-income dots illuminate for comparison.]

Now shift to a country that should, on wealth alone, be difficult to place below the line.

In 2010, the United States records life expectancy around 78.5 years, while the income-based expectation is about 81.1.

The point is not that the U.S. is low-income and constrained. It is one of the richest countries in the dataset period.

And within a tight band of similarly wealthy countries, the U.S. sits near the bottom on life expectancy.

[VISUAL: Time scrubber for U.S. life-expectancy gap, 2006 -> 2015.]

If this were only a one-year anomaly, the gap would quickly close.

It does not.

The U.S. life-expectancy distance from expectation is below the line in 2006, still below in 2010, and farther below again by 2015.

That gives us a decade pattern, not a snapshot.

[VISUAL: Small-multiple panel. U.S. vs high-income peer averages for maternal, infant, under-5 change from 2006 to 2015. Canada highlighted as one named comparator.]

The same pattern appears in child and maternal signals.

U.S. maternal mortality is flat at 14.0 in 2006 and 14.0 in 2015, while high-income peers decline on average.

U.S. infant mortality improves over the decade, but by less than the average high-income peer reduction.

U.S. under-5 mortality also improves, but again more slowly than peer averages.

One concrete comparator: Canada moves maternal mortality from 9.0 to 7.0 over the same period.

None of this means income is irrelevant. The U.S. still has resources most countries do not.

It means money and delivery are different things.

A country can spend heavily while the care system remains fragmented.
Coverage can expand while access still varies by geography, cost, and continuity.
A wealthy country can spend heavily and still leave avoidable gaps in prevention and early intervention.

In this chart language, that entire institutional complexity shows up as one simple geometric fact: the dot stays below the line.

[VISUAL: U.S. fades. Equatorial Guinea locks in below line with expanded gap bars. Sierra Leone floor annotation appears.]

At the opposite end of the distribution, Equatorial Guinea shows how large the negative distance can become.

In 2010, life expectancy is about 55.9 years against an income-based expectation near 73.7.

That is not a small miss. It is a structural miss.
It is the kind of distance no income ranking can justify on its own.

Supporting indicators move in the same direction: infant and under-5 mortality are far worse than income-based expectation in the same year.

Then the floor appears.

Sierra Leone records maternal mortality of 1,630 per 100,000 in 2010.
That marks the floor of this distribution in 2010.

Different country profile.
Different income path.
Same failure.

[VISUAL: Return to full global scatter. Year slider appears. Distribution strip updates from 2006 to 2015.]

So what moves these gaps?

From 2006 to 2015, global distance from the line compresses somewhat:
median absolute gap moves from about 3.1 years to about 2.7,
90th-percentile distance drops from about 9.3 to about 7.3,
and mean absolute distance also declines.

So the world in this dataset gets somewhat less dispersed relative to income expectation.

But that compression is partial, not transformational.

Large positive and negative divergences remain present in every year. The distribution does not collapse around zero.

This is not an abstract prompt. In this data, upward movers usually do not improve in only one place. They improve across connected survival pathways: maternal care, infant outcomes, under-5 outcomes, and life expectancy moving together rather than in isolation.

That pattern suggests system-wide improvement, not a single isolated intervention.

A useful way to read the chart is to ask three operational questions.

First, can people enter care early and reliably through primary care rather than only at crisis point?

Second, when higher-acuity care is needed, do referral chains actually connect local facilities to district and hospital capacity in time?

Third, does financing protect continuity, or do cost shocks force households to delay care until conditions are harder and more expensive to treat?

Those are not ideological questions. They are system design questions, and they are measurable in outcomes over time.

The contrast across these countries makes that concrete.

Rwanda's movement above expectation is accompanied by broad frontline improvement.
The U.S. remains below expectation over a decade despite high aggregate resources.
Equatorial Guinea sits far below expectation, and Sierra Leone shows what the floor can look like when care systems fail.

None of this yields a universal recipe that copies cleanly from one country to another.

But it does narrow where to look: conversion capacity, referral reliability, and continuity in maternal-child pathways are where gains are most likely to compound.

That persistence is the governance burden: which arrangements move dots upward, which arrangements stall despite resources, and which reforms reduce the deadliest outcomes at the bottom.
You can watch that burden accumulate or ease, year by year, on the same line.

The hopeful reading is not that progress is automatic. It is that progress is governable.

What happens next is a governance question, not a math question.

## Methods Endnote (Single Block)

- Scope: 217 countries, 2006-2015, using the transformed MDG-aligned dataset in `website/data/`.
- Core frame: country-year outcomes are compared to income-based expectations; narrative emphasis is on distance from the life-expectancy expectation line.
- This is descriptive, not causal. Residual gaps identify over- and under-performance relative to income, but do not isolate mechanisms.
- Country averages can hide within-country inequality and measurement limits.
- The narrative prioritizes high-coverage health indicators (life expectancy, maternal mortality, infant mortality, under-5 mortality).
- External context lines (definitions, primary-care framing, Rwanda reconstruction context, high-income system variation context) are documented in `docs/story_context_endnotes_v5.md`.
